## The Origin Story

_This is the founding myth of the practice. It is the most important thing the About page communicates._

At age 12, Sofia immigrated to Israel. The rupture of it — losing familiar ground, rebuilding identity in a new language — became the defining experience of her professional life. She didn't come to therapy through a textbook. She came through a child who needed that kind of holding, and understood somewhere early that her personal pain would become a bridge to others in their own moments of crisis.

This origin matters twice over:
1. It explains why she shows up the way she does — with full presence, without distance, remembering the small things.
2. It directly mirrors the lived experience of her Russian-speaking clients. For them, she is not a therapist who "works with immigrants." She is someone who knows that terrain from the inside.

**Do not flatten this story into a credential. It is the center of the About page.**

Not her credentials (good, but not the point). Not her modalities (integrative — many are). The difference is:

1. **She is fully present as a human being in the room.** Not behind the professional glass. She laughs with clients, feels what they feel, shows up as a person. This is rare and specific and it is the thing clients name when they describe her.

## Target Audience

### Who She Works With
Individual therapy for adolescents (from 16), young adults, adults, and elderly. Also provides parent guidance alongside youth therapy.

**Areas:** Trauma and PTSD · Grief, loss, and life crises · Depression · Anxiety · HSP (Highly Sensitive People) · ADHD · LGBTQ+ community · Relationship difficulties · Immigration and identity

### The Ideal Client (Hebrew audience)
She or he has been carrying something alone for a while. Not necessarily in crisis — but at a point where the weight has become visible, the old ways of coping have stopped working, and the idea of talking to someone who will actually listen has become more possible than it once was. They've probably waited longer than they should. They're a little afraid it won't help, a little afraid it will.

### The Ideal Client (Russian-speaking audience)
She or he immigrated to Israel, possibly years ago, possibly recently. They've managed. They've built something. But there are layers that never got processed — the identity rupture, the grief of a life left behind, things that couldn't quite be said in Hebrew. They want to work in Russian, with someone who doesn't need an explanation of how complicated it all is.

**Option C — Sofia's presence, her specific qualities**
> כאן, לא שוכחים שום פרט — ולא ממהרים לשום מקום.

_"Here, nothing is forgotten — and there's no hurry to get anywhere."_
Works for: pairing with a subhead that names the client's situation. Anchored in Sofia's documented differentiators (memory, unhurried pace). Different angle — about what she offers rather than what the client is feeling.

## Tone of Voice

### The Site Should Sound Like
Warm. Direct. Unhurried. Specific. Human. Sofia speaking to a person who walked in the door — not a practice brochure describing services.

### Five Adjectives
Present · Warm · Grounded · Honest · Careful

### The Site Should NEVER Sound Like
- Clinical distance ("presenting complaint," "therapeutic alliance," "evidence-based modalities")
- Motivational energy ("Transform your life!" "You deserve to thrive!")
- Generic therapy language that could be on any website in the country
- Anything written to rank on Google
- The word "journey" used metaphorically

### Hebrew Tone Note
Direct and personal. She speaks to "you" (את/אתה), not "the client." Not overly formal Hebrew — the register is warm and conversational. Sentences are short and specific, not poetic or elaborate. This is Sofia talking, not a website talking about Sofia.

### Russian Tone Note
Slightly more formal register than Hebrew (culturally appropriate), but with genuine warmth underneath. The Russian version is not a translation of the Hebrew — it is Sofia speaking Russian. For the Russian-speaking community, the tone signals safety: this is a professional, yes, but one who shares the cultural landscape. References to the immigrant experience should feel matter-of-fact, not sentimentalized.
